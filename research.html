Chiraltronics: Material, Transport, and Device

Quantum Hall Effect, Nonlinear Transport, Chirality-Induced Spin and Magnetic Effects.
Chiral quantum materials—those lacking inversion or mirror symmetry, are a rapidly emerging frontier in condensed matter physics due to their ability to host exotic quantum phenomena inaccessible in centrosymmetric systems. Their symmetry-breaking nature enables novel control over spin, charge, and heat transport, paving the way for energy-efficient logic, spintronics, and hybrid quantum technologies.

By combining precise device fabrication with low-temperature measurements, we explore topological and quantum properties of electrons intrinsic to chiral materials.

Oxide Semiconductors & Thin-Film Devices

Atomic-layer oxides, IGZO, and high-mobility channels for robust electronics.
We develop and study oxide semiconductor thin films such as ALD-grown InOx and IGZO for next-generation display backplanes, micro-LED drivers, and backend-of-line compatible logic. Atomic layer deposition offers precise thickness control and excellent uniformity, enabling large-area integration on flexible and unconventional substrates.

Our research focuses on interface engineering, defect and trap physics, and long-term stability under electrical and thermal stress. By combining process optimization with device characterization, we aim to achieve high mobility, low contact resistance, and excellent reliability in oxide thin-film transistors.

Monolithic Integration & 3D Nanoelectronics

Stacking devices and materials for compact, heterogeneous systems.
Monolithic 3D integration allows multiple tiers of devices to be vertically stacked on the same wafer, dramatically increasing functional density beyond traditional planar scaling. We explore oxide, 2D, and other low-temperature materials as add-on device layers compatible with CMOS back-end processes.

Our work includes the design and demonstration of stacked transistors, integrated logic and memory elements, and driver circuits for applications such as micro-LEDs, sensors, and cryo-compatible readout electronics. We study thermal budgets, inter-tier coupling, and reliability of these complex integrated systems.

Cryogenic & Future Electronics

Electronics that operate at extreme temperatures and interface with quantum systems.
Future computing systems will increasingly integrate classical control electronics with quantum processors and sensors operating at cryogenic temperatures. We investigate materials and device architectures that maintain stable performance across a wide temperature range, from cryogenic regimes to high-temperature operation.

By characterizing transport, noise, and reliability at low temperatures, we aim to identify device concepts and material combinations that are promising for cryo-CMOS, quantum control, and sensing front-ends, while remaining compatible with large-scale integration.
